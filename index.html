From Traditional BI
to NeuGBI

How business intelligence evolves from static dashboards to iterative, question-driven exploration.

Background

What is Traditional
Business Intelligence?

BI helps organizations turn raw data into actionable insights. It works beautifully — when you already know exactly what question to ask.

1
Frame Define the question.
2
Shape Organize data into a model.
3
Read Interpret results and decide.
Traditional BI is like assembling a puzzle when you already know the picture on the box.
A Concrete Example

How Traditional BI Answers
a Single Question

Suppose a researcher asks: "How has Russian publication output changed in recent years?"
Here is how traditional BI would handle it.

1
Communicate Requirements

The analyst meets with domain experts to clarify the question: Which data sources? What time range? What counts as "Russian"?

Analyst ↔ Domain Expert ↔ Database Admin — multiple rounds of discussion before any data is touched.

2
Build the Wide Table

The BI analyst constructs a wide table by joining Publication, Organization, and other related tables, filtered by country and year.

Publication ⨝ Organization
This step is manual, time-consuming, and requires deep schema knowledge.

3
Analyze & Report

Finally, the analyst aggregates, slices by year, and produces a trend chart. One question answered.

But what if the result raises a new question? The entire pipeline restarts from Step 1.

Traditional BI can answer one known question well. But real investigation is rarely just one question — each answer leads to the next.
Case Study

BI in Action: Russia-Ukraine
& Russian Academia

"What is the impact of the Russia-Ukraine conflict on Russian scientific research?"

1
Measure Output

Count Russian publications over time. Found a steep decline starting 2022.

2
Explore Funding

Add funding data. The European Commission stopped funding Russian projects after 2022.

3
Compare Partners

Add a second organization layer. Compared EC support for Russia vs. Ukraine.

Each round produced a new question. But each round also required rebuilding the data setup from scratch.
Finding 1

Russian Academic Output
Has Collapsed

2021 (Pre-War) 8,976 papers
→
2024 (Post-War) 4,686 papers
Change -47.8%

Is this a global trend? No. US and Germany declined ~27.6%, while China grew +14.8%. Russia's extra ~20pp decline is war-specific.

The decline is accelerating. YoY: -6.0% (2022), -4.8% (2023), then -39.4% in 2024 — cumulative, compounding effects.

Finding 2

International Collaboration
Nearly Collapsed

US-Russia -92.5% 3,614 → 271 papers
Germany-Russia -93.0% 656 → 46 papers
China-Russia -80.4% 570 → 112 papers
Collab Share of Output 10% → <5% of Russia's total output

Collaboration didn't just decline — it nearly vanished. International co-authorship, once a lifeline for Russian science, has been severed with virtually all major partners.

Finding 3 — Key Insight

The Turkey Surprise

A non-Western country with no sanctions against Russia dropped more than the sanctioning countries.

Turkey (No Sanctions) -98.4%
Germany (Sanctions) -93.0%

This proves sanctions alone cannot explain the collapse. The fundamental driver is academic platform isolation — international databases reduced visibility of Russian papers, affecting collaboration with ALL countries.

Root Cause Analysis

The Causal Chain

The investigation revealed a multi-layered mechanism, not a simple cause-and-effect.

⚠
Russia-Ukraine War
→
⛝

+ Academic platform isolation
→
🔗

With ALL countries

High dependence = high vulnerability. Turkey, with the highest pre-war reliance on Russian collaboration (8.74% of output), suffered the worst decline (-87.9% in collaboration ratio).

Platform isolation is the fundamental cause. International databases (OpenAIRE, Crossref, publishers) systematically reduced Russian paper visibility — this is sanction-agnostic and affects all partners equally.

This kind of multi-layered, hypothesis-driven investigation is exactly what traditional BI struggles to support.
The Challenge

Where Traditional BI
Breaks Down

The workflow is too rigid for questions that keep evolving.

01

Requires knowing the answer path in advance

If the analyst misses one important factor early on, the entire process must be rebuilt from scratch.

02

The schema is fixed and rigid

Exploratory questions change shape as the investigation moves forward, but the data model cannot follow.

03

No reusability between rounds

The Publication–Organization join was computed 3 separate times in the Russia-Ukraine analysis. Each new question restarts the same groundwork.

04

Repeated computation costs add up

The workflow keeps paying the cost of data preparation instead of spending time on the next insight.

Core Contribution

Making Exploration Feasible:
Sampling-Based Operators

Iterative exploration means running Source and Join over and over. Both hit performance walls — sampling tears them down.

Bottleneck 1 — Source

Subgraph matching is NP-complete. Finding a 4-path on a tiny 9,892-vertex graph already takes ~4 minutes — far from interactive.

Bottleneck 2 — Join Explosion

Merging hypergraphs blows up fast. On OpenAIRE, a single citation join yields 1.4 billion rows — and exploration keeps joining again.

Key insight. BI analysts care about aggregates (COUNT, SUM, trends), not individual records. If the aggregate can be accurately estimated, the full result set is unnecessary.
S
Source-Sample

Adapt FaSTest tree sampling for subgraph matching — returns a uniform sample instead of enumerating every match.

J
Join-Sample

Weighted group-sample on one side, then one match per sampled hyperedge — equivalent to uniform sampling of the full join result.

✓
Unbiased End-to-End

We prove any composition of Source-Sample and Join-Sample still yields a uniform sample — so COUNT/SUM estimates remain unbiased across long exploratory chains.

36.7× end-to-end speedup vs. no-sample
<1% error at 1M samples on SF1
